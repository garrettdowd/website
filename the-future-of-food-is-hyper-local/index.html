The Future of Food is Hyper-local

August 19, 2020

The image below is the first result on Google for the search term "agriculture". It is also what first comes to mind for most people. However, traditional soil culture methods alone do not meet the needs of a growing world population and growing urban centers. Incremental improvement and corporate mergers are not sustainable solutions for the future of our food.

If you haven't already read my article, Why Food Sucks, this would be a great time. This is the second article in a three part series on automated urban farming. As mentioned in the previous article, a possible "radical" solution to our growing food problems is to rewind the clock and return to a more decentralized production model using the latest scientific understanding of topics from the biology of plants to the quantum physics of LEDs.

Hyper-local food production is such a vision for the future. In fact, you may already be purchasing food from one of the many hyper-local farms including Bowery, Green City Growers, BrightFarms, Great Lakes Growers, Plenty, and Aerofarms.

Hyper-local Food Production

Hyper-local food production at commercial scales generally aims to solve two main problems posed in Why Food Sucks;

  1. Transportation
  2. Chemical and pharmaceutical use

Removing these problems from the equation enables;

  1. Cultivars chosen for flavor/nutrition
  2. Food harvested closer to peak flavor/nutrition
  3. Reduced emissions from transportation
  4. Reduced number of middle men (more supply chain transparency, less corruption)
  5. Reduced ecological damage from pesticides (save the bees!)
  6. Reduce possible chronic disease caused by chemical use
  7. More "in-tune" and responsive to community needs/wants

This is ironic to me because, in this narrow perspective, food production was better a century ago than it is now.

So what does hyper-local food production actually look like anyway? 

Hyper-local food production can range from personal gardens to large indoor vertical farms.

The more decentralized and diverse food production is the more resistant it is to any single source of failure (disease, drought, destruction, etc). It is important to have a range of production scales.

Commercial Scale

There are a lot of people that need to eat. Modern controlled environment agriculture (CEA) science allows us to grow produce more quickly, in higher densities, with better water efficiency than ever before. Hydroponics is a fast developing field of agricultural science that is at the heart of the commercial scale operations seen above. The most popular hydroponic techniques used at a commercial scale are

  • Vertical and Roof Farms
    • Aeroponics
    • Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) a.k.a. Shallow Water Culture (SWC)
  • Greenhouses
    • Deep Water Culture (DWC)
    • Substrate-based
      • Ebb and Flow a.k.a. Flood and Drain
      • Drip Irrigation

Hydroponics is generally defined in the United States as growing plants without soil (including non-soil substrates like rockwool). This means that macro and micro nutrients that normally come from the soil or fertilizers must be provided through other means. Many hydroponic operations use nutrient dosing (adding refined macro/micro nutrient concentrates) to easily create the proper conditions for optimal growth.

However there are more natural ways to grow produce hydroponically. One such method is called aquaponics.

Aquaponics is a hydroponic growing method where fish and plants are grown symbiotically, mimicking the nitrogen cycle in nature.

Fish produce waste which are converted to nitrates by bacteria. The plants then clean the water for the fish by uptaking nitrates and other macro/micro nutrients. You can read more about aquaponics in the next article.

Community Scale

Humans have been foraging and cultivating food forever. Finding food, growing food, and harvesting food all have deeply ingrained reward mechanisms that evolved over tens of thousands of years. Communities that grew food together survived together. It would be biologically ignorant to disregard the social benefits of community gardens. 

Community gardens also help educate people about food. What does good/fresh food actually taste like? What varieties exist? How do you cook it? These are important questions that can encourage commercial scale operations to produce a wider variety of produce, which ultimately provides a more comprehensive source of nutrition. While community gardens can be difficult for a working community to manage and upkeep, there are design methodologies that could help, such as permaculture. Permaculture is a fascinating area of biomimetic agricultural design to dive into if sustainability and ecology interest you.

Ultimately, community gardens need to start from within the community and personal gardens are a great way to start.

Personal Scale

Growing food at home has many of the same benefits as community gardens. Growing food at home is not about reducing food costs. It is about flavor, variety, a sense of accomplishment, and a connection with nature. However, growing food at home can be difficult for busy families and urban dwellers, especially at a scale that provides more than a handful of tomatoes or a single annual bushel of basil.

Luckily, there are a few options to reduce the burden of managing a home garden (micro-farm). For traditional outdoor or greenhouse soil-culture there is the open-source Farmbot (I am a huge fan), which automatically seeds, waters, and weeds an outdoor grow bed. There are also hydroponic systems that are designed for indoor use such as Seedo and Grobo. Unsurprisingly, these startups are focused on the cannabis market and should also work well for other fruiting plants like tomato and pepper. However, they are not great designs for serious leafy green and herb production.

As a cooking/food enthusiast and also a busy urban dweller, I sought a solution that would allow me to grow enough produce for daily meals..... inside my 700 sqft third-floor apartment.
