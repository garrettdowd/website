uFarm | Product

September 2, 2020

Growing your own food can be as simple as a plant in a glass of water. However, a glass of water did not solve my food growing problems. So, I designed and built an automated, apartment-sized, aquaponic grow system that I released as an open-source project on Github. For the motivation behind this project check out my article, Why Food Sucks.

This is the first article in a series about the uFarm product design process. The series will delve into three distinct areas of the design process;

  1. Product | Building a useful product that actually solves problems.
  2. Aquaponics | Plant science turned into design parameters. (Coming mid-September)
  3. Engineering | Iterative design of a cyber-physical system. (Coming late September)

The series will end with;

  1. Outcomes | Running and testing the prototype.
  2. Build Log | Pictures and comments from the process.

Social Impact Disclaimer

uFarm is built with the intent for social impact. It attempts to solve real user problems/pains in a way that tackles systemic problems with the food system. In many ways it is a low cost experiment to assess the viability of a social startup.

The organic movement is a good example of what uFarm is trying to achieve. Without the work of film makers and non-profits, consumers would have no reason to choose organic produce at the shop or eat at a farm to table restaurant. While the organic movement has become overly commercialized, it has at least moved the public to be more conscious of what they eat.

Product

What problems does uFarm solve? Who is it for? How is it strategically positioned? What features should be built first? These are all part of the product vision that inform engineering design choices. While uFarm is an experimental side project that is not meant to be commercialized, it is meant to have value. Product and user focused thinking helped me to test more valuable hypotheses with the initial uFarm prototype.

Problems

Taking startup methods cues from Steve Blank and Eric Ries, uFarm is based on a number of hypotheses about the problem, user, and solution. In my opinion, startups in the personal food growing space often fail because they do not identify painful problems. Rather, they create new problems including maintenance and living space clutter. uFarm is focused on common problems that are shared by anyone who cooks food at home.

  1. Food spoilage
    1. A surprising amount of food waste occurs because food portions sold at groceries are too large or because you didn't get around to making that basil pesto as soon as you had hoped. This is especially true for people that do not live with a partner or family.
  2. Time
    1. It is taco night, how often have you forgotten the cilantro/corriander and had to take a quick run to the shop. Or, more realistically, you have taco supplies at home and there is no way that you are venturing outside of your house to buy fresh produce.
  3. Cannot purchase at grocer
    1. For culinary enthusiasts it can be hard to find ethnic or regional varieties of herbs. Kaffir lime, curry leaves, holy/thai basil, birdseye chilis, and even lemongrass can be hard to find. Because these herbs are less popular they sit on shelves for longer periods and quickly loose their flavor and/or spoil before they make it into your mouth.
  4. Sustainability and production transparency
    1. What you choose to eat has a large impact on the environment and people's lives. At the end of the day, certifications and labels like USDA Organic or Free-Trade Certified are ultimately stacks of paperwork that the consumer can neither view nor verify.

At a societal level there are additional problems. A quick overview of these problems were given from a system perspective in my article, Why Food Sucks. However, I want to reframe these problems with a user perspective.

  • Cooking is hard
    • Not everyone is a Ferran Adria or a Nathan Myhrvold. Many American families no longer pass down a plethora of family recipes for daily consumption. There are too many "influencer chefs" that give garbage recipes and poor cooking techniques. Deciding on what to cook and knowing how to cook it is too large of a barrier for many, especially if what you cook tastes no better than your local Chipotle.
    • It takes a lot of time to go and buy fresh ingredients, especially if you live in a food desert.
    • It takes even more time to plan meals so that food does not spoil in your fridge.

 

  • You are good at tasting for sugar, not nutrition
    • That delicious apple at your local grocer was probably in storage for eight or more months. During that time it lost most of its nutritional value and is now just a convenient ball of sugar water. But hey it still tastes good right?
    • That pint of cherry tomatoes you just got, yeah it was picked before it was ripe and then artificially ripened with ethylene gas during transportation. It doesn't have the same nutritional value as tomatoes allowed to naturally ripen on the plant.
    • John Hopkins on the marketing and labeling of food

 

  • No one taught me
    • Home economic classes have been killed off. Both parents have full time jobs. Food is bought in plastic containers. Potatoes grow on trees like tomatoes, right?
    • How we eat is heavily influenced by family, friends, and society.

 

  • Out of sight, out of mind
    • The rain forest is being destroyed so you can eat your donuts and potato chips made with palm oil (often just listed as vegetable oil).
    • Cattle farming. So many problems here from more deforestation to animal abuse.
    • Human abuse. You really don't want to know how your tea and coffee is produced. It is quite ironic that American companies now support behavior which the Country fought against for independence, [cough] Boston Tea Party.

Wow. Those are a lot of problems. Hopefully the next sections are easier reads.

Users

Passion for an idea can easily lead to feature creep and a lack of user focus. If the product is socially important then everyone should use it right? While widespread adoption may be the goal for the greatest social impact, product success does not grow on trees. Again taking cues from the tech startup world, Geoffrey Moore and his book Crossing the Chasm offer a commonly used framework to think about user categories and market adoption. This section will focus on

  1. Innovators
  2. Early Adopters

The ideal innovator is both a tech and plant geek. There aren't many of us. We have a broad understanding of electronics/computers and love to build rather than talk. Each of us are interesting in plants for different reasons. Some of us found a love for plants through food, some of us grew up working in gardens or on farms, and some just want to grow plants on spaceships for interstellar journeys. The innovator has an account on Github and uses it to try out projects that other people have created. The innovator is not worried about blowing a fuse or fixing a leak.

The early adopter is not as technically literate. However, what they lack in technical skill they make up for in passion for fixing our food system. They are careful about what food they buy and who they buy it from. They attend their local farmers market and they probably have a small garden at home. They may subscribe to food philosophies such as veganism, but they understand that these philosophies are about principles and not about rules. They are looking to do more, but time is a limiting factor.

Business Environment

Competition

uFarm is not the first automated home grow system. Grove Ecosystem is a failed startup that raised over $4M USD from high profiles investors like Tim Ferris in 2016. Grove struggled with manufacturing and seemed to fail at attracting early adopters. There are many smaller countertop aquaponic systems like EcoGarden and Aquasprouts, however these are extremely small and simple solutions that are more of a display piece than a useful tool. There are also automated grow boxes that rely on nutrient dosing such as Grobo, Seedo, and Leaf. Most of the automated hydroponic systems focus on growing cannabis, which seems to be one of the only profitable target markets at the moment.

There are very few open-source hydroponic projects. The most famous project is the publicly criticized and recently closed OpenAg project at MIT which created the Personal Food Computer. 

There are also projects aimed at automating traditional soil culture such as Farmbot, a personal favorite.

Technology Trends

uFarm relies on the success and further development of a few core technologies.

  • Light Emitting Diodes | Hail Haitz
  • Sensors (pH, EC, DO, lux, etc) | Cost, robustness
  • Cameras | Cost, wavelength
  • Processors | Moore please
  • Web Tech | edge-computing and user interface capabilities
  • Machine Learning | tools to automatically assess plant health problems

Regulatory

The regulatory environment is non-prohibitive and there are a number of legislative opportunities that would be extremely beneficial including;

  • Environmental Protection, Carbon Tax
  • Supply Chain Transparency
  • Agricultural Chemical Use Restrictions
  • Redistribution of agricultural subsidies to better support small/local farmers

Product Specs

While these aren't detailed product specs that would be used for Jira/Confluence, the following should give a good idea of how product design is prioritized. These product specs are for an MVP targeting innovators and do not represent the specs for the original prototype.

  1. Hardware
    1. Footprint less than 10 ft2 or 1 m2
    2. Autonomous for a minimum of 1 month.
    3. Bimonthly maintenance/cleaning.
    4. Ability to grow any leafy green (vegetative growth)
    5. Production rate of 3 kilograms per month
    6. Off the shelf components, locally source-able
    7. Build time of 12 hours
    8. Limit tools to drill, saw, knife, wire stripper, and soldering pencil.
  2. Software
    1. Use existing open-source projects
    2. Extensible
      1. Easily customize automation
      2. Integrate with smart home
      3. Community grow recipes
    3. Remote Management
    4. One click install

Trade-offs

At a system level, there are a number of major decisions to make, each with their own (and sometimes coupled) trade-offs.

Aquaponic vs Nutrient Dosing

ProsCons
  • Aquaponics is inherently more stable (pH, nutrients), reducing the chance of plant death
  • Nutrient dosing allows for greater control over growing conditions, including flowering/fruit growth
  • Aquaponics relies on establishing a bacterial colony
  • Nutrient dosing is costly to automate and is sensitive to sensor drift which increases maintenance requirements

Size

LargerSmaller
  • Grow more food
  • Less sensitive to wide swings in growing conditions
  • More customization and expandable
  • Takes up less space
  • Smaller initial investment
  • Less setup time

Automation

MoreLess
  • Reduce maintenance
  • Measure and optimize growing
  • Cheaper
  • Simpler, smaller number of components that could fail

Customizability vs Simplicity

With any product there is an inherent trade-off between customizability and simplicity. Android vs Apple, API vs web interface, blank notebook vs planner, etc. The clear initial design directive here is modularity. As seen in the sections above, there are clear engineering trade-offs for size and automation. Designing with these modularity parameters in mind can allow the product to shift in response to user demands.
