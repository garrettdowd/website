How to Never Kill Another Plant

August 19, 2020

Over-watering is the #1 cause of houseplant death.

What if I told you there was a way to never worry about watering your plants again, even when absent for months at a time. What if I told you there is a way to grow plants twice as fast with no synthetic fertilizer? This article will discuss how create a self-sustaining indoor grow system that requires very little maintenance and can provide a surprisingly large amount of food.

In the previous two articles Why Food Sucks and The Future of Food is Hyper-local, I reviewed critical issues with our current food system and potential solutions to those issues. Growing your own food is a small, but important piece of the solution to building a healthier, more sustainable food system. Using the scientific understanding of hydroponics and engineering principles from system design and automation, growing your own food can be a realistic goal even for time-constrained urban dwellers. This is the idea behind the uFarm open-source project. Learn more about how I designed uFarm.

uFarm

uFarm uses principles from aquaponics to design a self-sustaining micro-ecosystem that can grow plants for weeks to months without any intervention or maintenance.

I left the system running autonomously for more than a month while travelling and I came back to plants that had quadrupled in size.

Aquaponics is a way of providing nutrients to plants that mimics a natural ecosystem cycle.

  1. Fish are fed (possibly with food scraps)
  2. Fish produce waste (ammonia)
  3. Nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia to nitrites and then nitrates
  4. Plants uptake nitrates, in effect cleaning the water for the fish

A variety of hydroponic techniques can be applied to aquaponics. The initial uFarm prototype uses flood & drain media beds as well as deep water culture, however nutrient film technique or aeroponics can also be designed into an aquaponic system.

How uFarm Works

uFarm is not just a simple aquaponic system, it is a highly automated, connected, and integrated system. It was designed using a variety of engineering tools from Darcy-Weisbach fluid flow equations to multi-physics modelling for over-constrained mechanical loading conditions. The entire system is highly coupled, which is engineering speak that means if you change the size/design/specification of one component then it changes multiple other system component requirements. For simplicity, much of the high-level parametric design was carried out in a spreadsheet that could be used to design systems that are larger or smaller. Don't worry, I am not going into technical detail in this article. However, if you want to dive into the design and engineering process then head to my article on Designing uFarm.

 The idea behind uFarm is to hide a lot of the difficult engineering and biology behind easy to use design tools and user interfaces.

To get a better sense of what goes into a uFarm system take a look at the system diagram below. This system is fully recirculating and contains enough water to run for more than a month (accounting for evaporation and plant uptake). In fact, this system requires less maintenance than a normal fish tank because the plants are doing the heavy lifting of removing excess nitrates from the system.

  1. Water is first pumped to the top fish tank from the bottom DWC containers. The pump is controlled by the system's computer which regulates the watering cycles.
  2. Water from the fish tank then overflows into the media beds in the middle. The media beds serve a variety of purposes. It traps physical fish waste particles and also houses the majority of the nitrifying bacteria that breaks down ammonia into nitrates.
  3. The media beds are designed as a flood and drain system. Each bed has an electronically activated direct action valve and water sensor that controls when the water from the bed is drained into the DWC container below.

The Growing Edge of Plant Research

NASA has been a leader in hydroponic science (it's the best way to grow food in space thanks to its recirculating design). While there is a good amount of fundamental science that understands how "root zone" conditions affect plant growth, there are still a huge number of growth parameter questions. Each plant has it's own specific environmental condition "recipes" that optimize growth and specific chemical compound concentrations that affect flavor and more.

Nutrient concentrations, pH, dissolved oxygen, temperature, light spectral power distributions, humidity, CO2 concentrations are all parameters that affect plant growth. These parameters vary over time and influence plant growth. For example, many fruiting plants require shorter light cycles with more red light to enter a flowering/fruiting stage.

If you enter the Google Scholar hydroponics rabbit hole (like I did) you will find journal articles that study specific growth parameters for specific species of plants. There is no unified theory that can fully optimize growth for any plant. As a science nerd, this really gets me going. I get to play with knowledge at the edge of scientific understanding... and I get to eat the results afterwards!

uFarm is built with this experimentation in mind. The system is capable of programmatically changing growing parameters such as light intensity, light spectral distribution, water cycles, humidity, and temperature. In other words, it can follow tried and tested grow recipes or it help you experiment and create your own.

How to Design Your Own uFarm

uFarm is more than just a single design as seen in pictures above. It aims to be a set of open-source design tools and software infrastructure that can help anyone create an automated hydroponic/aquaponic system that fits their specific needs (size, cost, types of plants).

Unfortunately that goal is still a few years in the future. In the short-term, the project aims to offer more "base-models" or specific size configurations that will fit most users' needs. If you are technically included (hardware or software) then head over to the uFarm Github page and give it a follow. If you are interested in checking out the build log for the prototype system then check out my article on Designing uFarm.

Overall, the system worked amazingly well for a prototype. It kept me fed with seriously good tasting herbs and required almost zero effort/time. However, there was a lot of learning from the initial prototype.

  1. Building a custom glass fish tank was stupid hard for an inexperienced caulker. In my opinion, the aesthetic improvement was not worth the effort.
  2. The piping design needs to be modified to reduce noise. Gurgling water is quite loud in a small apartment.
  3. The system was too large for a single person. I was making basil pesto so often that I had to give it away to friends.
  4. Media beds were too time intensive to build and tear down. Would probably design a NFT/Drip Irrigation level with a separate bio and particle filter.
  5. DWC bed was difficult to keep light tight. Light exposure causes excess algae growth which increases maintenance. This will also be an issue for aeroponics and NFT systems.
  6. The intensity and color of the light can be disruptive to a living area. Automated or adjustable shrouds would be a great addition.
