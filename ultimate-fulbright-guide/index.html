Where to Start?

  1. The best place to start is the official Fulbright website. I highly recommend reading through all of the "About" sections. Most questions can be answered there. The Eligibility page is also an important read (spoiler: you can't yet have a doctorate degree but you must have a bachelor degree by the start of the grant period)
  2. If you are currently a student or are recently graduated the next best place to start is with your university's Fulbright Program Advisor (FPA). The FPA is often a faculty member dedicated to helping students with the application process. Do not discount their experience and knowledge about the general process! You can use the Fulbright website to search for your university's FPA. They will probably appreciate it if you read the info on the official website first üòÅ
  3. Read the rest of this post.
  4. Check out current and past Fulbright Alumni Ambassadors and reach out! Alternatively, find the person who had the Award you are interested (Linkedin and Google are good starting points, or use the Fulbright Grantee Directory)
  5. Check out Recorded Webinars with detailed application information.

What is Fulbright?

Since you already know the basics I am going to delve into some of the complexities of the program to provide better context.¬†

  • The Fulbright umbrella actually contains multiple academic-based programs
    • the Fulbright U.S. Student Program, for students and professionals
    • the Fulbright Scholar Program, for university faculty
    • other specific awards, see the full list on the State Department website
  • The Fulbright Programs are partially funded by annual appropriations from Congress and are housed under the Department of State, specifically the Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs (ECA). However, ECA contracts the administration of the program to The Institute of International Education. Other funding (less than 50% as of 2019) comes from partner countries and institutions. However some Fulbright Awards are fully financed by partner countries or institutions.
    • This means that the program is funded at the discretion of Congress and under the policy direction of the Executive Branch. The President appoints the J. William Fulbright Foreign Scholarship Board (FFSB).
      • Fulbright has long received bipartisan support from Congress because the program has a concrete impact in both developing leaders and increasing "soft power" worldwide. It is seen as one of the most powerful peace keeping tools and is strongly supported by military generals.
    • Awards come and go as foreign affairs policy and relationships change (both in the U.S. and in partner countries). The availability of an Award may also be dependent on a specific institution.
  • Fulbright is a partnership program between the U.S. and 160+ countries. While IIE administers the project in the U.S., they rely on working with partner countries for both funding, implementation, and applications of the Fulbright programs.
    • Many of the largest partner countries (49) have commissions which oversee the program. A full list is here. While U.S. embassies oversee the program directly in many other countries.
    • This means that a foreign commission or a U.S. embassy will most likely make the final decision on your application.

ECA also houses other international exchange programs so be sure to check them out!!!

Benefits of Being a Fulbright Scholar

The website gives the tangible benefits of the Award which includes at minimum

  • round-trip transportation to the host country
    • A lump sum for you to book your own transportation
  • funding to cover room, board, and incidental costs
    • How generous this is depends on a variety of factors including cost of living. The expectation is to be funded at the level of an American graduate student (GRA/GTA), however some Awards provide more (or possibly less).
  • Federal government non-competitive eligibility (NCE) status for 12 months
    • great if you want to work at a federal agency (NASA/DOE/CIA/etc) after Fulbright

However, if these were the only benefits then there would be very little difference from GTA/GRA funding. Fulbright is such a prized award because of the country specific and intangible benefits that cannot be guaranteed nor freely received. I will provide examples from my own experience and also link to other Fulbrighter's posts as I find them.

Is Fulbright worth it?

Yes. It obviously depends on what you make of it, but Fulbright is unrivaled for the breadth of opportunities available. For specific countries and universities there are comparable privately funded opportunities e.g. Rhodes and Schwarzman.

How do I select an Award to apply for?

The bad news. You can only apply for one Award. The good news. You only have to apply for one Award. There three main strategies you can use to select the right Award, but first some context.

In an ideal world you might have entered your undergraduate career with your eyes set on Fulbright and you have your career planned out at least 10 years. In this case it would be easy to chose an Award that best fits your needs. I don't know of many people who live in that ideal world.

I did not learn of Fulbright until my third year of undergrad. I also did not have a clear ten or five year plan. I was the first engineer in my family and I had just started to get involved in startups and research commercialization. However, I was focused on getting more international experience and Fulbright seemed like the ideal opportunity. Finding the right Fulbright Award was the first challenge. If you are in the position of getting ready to apply in the next application cycle then there are a few ways to narrow down your search.

  • Language
    • A number of countries have language requirements. Do you speak a language other than English? Could you become proficient in a language before applying? Generally less people apply to the Awards that have language requirements.
  • Restricted Awards
    • There are a number of Awards that are for a specific area of study or research. Because Awards change on a yearly basis, this takes a lot of due diligence to search through opportunities. Again, less people generally apply for restricted Awards.
  • Statistics
    • If you are interested in a general Award or an English Teaching Assistant then it may be useful to look at application statistics. Fulbright publishes limited application statistics that may help determine which Awards are highly competitive. Be cautious as application statistics vary every year and a low number of applications may encourage more people to apply the following year.
  • Your Dream
    • Always wanted to study in London? IT'S A TRAP! I repeat, IT'S A TRAP! This is not a good way to choose an Award because it is not fundamentally based in what makes a strong application. The U.S. Government and its taxpayers are not funding you to go have a year long vacation in your dream destination. They expect qualified candidates that are pursuing professional goals.

That being said, there are three main categories of Awards that you can apply for.

  • Study
    • Obtain a [post-]graduate degree. Often tied to a thesis/dissertation.
  • Research
    • Start or complete a research objective.
  • English Teaching Assistant
    • Teach English. Lots of opportunities all around the world.

Generally, the English Teaching Assistant Awards are the least competitive, but also have the largest selection of countries to choose from.

How do I apply?

The mechanics of the application process are all provided on the Fulbright website.¬† Definitely check out the application checklists and the application timeline. The only additional advice I can give here is to start EARLY (6+ months), which is much easier said than done.

  • Determine where your application may be weak and gain more experience before the application deadline.
  • Get an initial draft ASAP and revise over multiple months. Give time for your thoughts to coagulate and mature.

Chances of getting a Fulbright scholarship?

There is no good answer to this. From my experience, the Fulbright application review process is more comprehensive and well-rounded than any university application process. However, it is still a selection process and competition varies from year to year.

  • Fight the impostor syndrome. Looking at current Fulbright profiles can be daunting, but I can assure you that most successful Fulbright applicants feel the same way.
  • Making a decision solely on application statistics will probably lead to a weak application. Make sure to focus on your passions or goals and let that guide your application decisions.

What makes a competitive application?

There is a lot that goes into making a Fulbright Application. Your best course of action is to

  1. read the official Fulbright guidance
  2. reach out to a Fulbright alumni for more nuanced guidance

Otherwise here are some thoughts off the top of my head (avoid idioms in your application).

  • Write for your audience
    • see below
  • Follow good story telling techniques, e.g.
    • Hook
    • Information chunking
    • Story structure
    • Authenticity/Honesty
  • Big goals
  • Relevant experience
  • Passion for the mission of The Fulbright Program

Audience

Be sure to understand the stages of application review and the audience for which you are writing the application. Generally this includes;

  • Campus Committee
    • Unless you put little effort into your application and it would reflect poorly on the university, your committee should recommend your for the Award.
  • IIE/U.S. Selection
    • Applications are reviewed by relevant university faculty and professionals. You need to make sure you can convince this reader that your application is important/relevant and you are competent in your field of expertise.
  • Foreign Country
    • Foreign Commission¬†
      • Applications are generally reviewed by accomplished individuals who volunteer their time to review applications. Fulbright alumni may also read your applications. Your application should tell a story that does not overwhelm these readers with too much technical jargon. Be wary of assumptions that may come across as culturally ignorant.

How do I improve my application?

  • Get feedback from relevant faculty/professionals
  • Talk to a recent Fulbright alumnus (many are willing to help!)
  • Gain cultural insights from citizens of the foreign country
  • Gain more relevant academic/professional experience
  • Apply for relevant awards/competitions
  • Get some experience living abroad
